# Math Level 1 – Operations as Transformations

## Module Snapshot

- **Track:** Math – Foundations  
- **Level:** 1  
- **Theme:** Operations as Transformations  
- **Core Idea:**  
  Arithmetic operations are controlled changes (transformations) on quantities. Most have an opposite (inverse) operation that undoes their effect.

### Module Goals

Learners will:

- See operations (add, subtract, multiply, divide, exponent, root) as transformations on quantity.  
- Understand inverse pairs:
  - Addition ↔ Subtraction  
  - Multiplication ↔ Division  
  - Exponentiation ↔ Roots  
- Connect operations to:
  - Real-life situations (money, time, recipes).  
  - Simple chemistry (changing amounts of substances).  
  - Early engineering ideas (scaling and balance).

## Lesson 1.1 – Addition & Subtraction as Opposite Transformations

**Lesson ID:** `math-l1-01-add-sub-transformations`

### 1. Lesson Snapshot

**Title**  
Addition & Subtraction as Opposite Transformations

**One-Sentence Summary**  
Addition and subtraction act as opposite transformations on a quantity, like doing and undoing a move on a balanced scale.

**Learning Objectives – Learners will be able to…**

- Describe addition as combining or increasing a quantity.  
- Describe subtraction as separating or decreasing a quantity.  
- Recognize subtraction as the inverse of addition in simple equations.  
- Use fact families to connect addition and subtraction.  
- Connect equation balance to the idea of Ma’at (balance / truth).

**Key Terms**

- **Operation** – A rule or action applied to numbers.  
- **Sum** – Result of addition.  
- **Difference** – Result of subtraction.  
- **Inverse operation** – An operation that undoes another.  
- **Balance** – Both sides of an equation represent the same quantity.

**Symbolic / Lore Terms (metaphor only)**

- **Ma’at** – Principle of balance and truth in Kemet (ancient Egypt).  
  - Source inspiration: _Supreme Mathematic – African Ma’at Magic – African Creative Energy_.  

> [Deeper Study – Ma’at & Equation Balance]  
> In Supreme Mathematic and Ma’at-centered writings, “balance” connects to
> truth, justice, and right measure. In Grimora, this symbolism is a metaphor:
> the **mathematical** rule is simply that both sides of an equation must
> represent the same quantity. The lore adds meaning, but does not change
> the math.

### 2. Narrative Hook

Short Kimia lab story:

- Apprentice adds drops to a glowing vial → brightness increases.  
- Apprentice removes drops → mixture returns to its original state.  
- Master explains: every action has a reverse; real power is knowing how to do **and** undo.

### 3. Prior Knowledge & Warm-Up

**Assumed Prior Knowledge**

- Can count whole numbers (at least up to 20).  
- Understands “more” vs “less.”  
- Recognizes `+`, `−`, and `=` symbols.

**Warm-Up Ideas**

- Quick mental questions: `3 + 2`, `7 − 1`, `5 + 4`.  
- “More or less?” prompts using small sets or dots.

---

### 4. Main Concept Explanation

#### 4.1 Intuitive Explanation

- Addition = joining or “charging up” a quantity.  
- Subtraction = taking away or “discharging” a quantity.  
- Use number line or bead visuals to show movement right (add) and left (subtract).

#### 4.2 Formal / Semi-Formal Explanation

- Generic forms:
  - `a + b = c` → add `b` to `a`.  
  - `a − b = c` → remove `b` from `a`.  
- Inverse relationship:
  - If `3 + 2 = 5`, then `5 − 2 = 3` and `5 − 3 = 2`.  
  - Fact families show the same small “transformation loop.”

> [Deeper Study – Fact Families as Transformation Loops]  
> You can think of a fact family (like 3, 2, and 5) as a small “loop” of
> transformations: adding moves you forward around the loop, subtracting
> moves you backward. In later algebra, this idea grows into solving
> equations by “undoing” operations step by step.

#### 4.3 Worked Examples

- Simple story contexts:
  - 5 coins + 2 coins → 7 coins (`5 + 2 = 7`).  
  - 7 coins − 2 coins → 5 coins (`7 − 2 = 5`).  
- Build 2–3 full fact families (e.g., 4, 3, 7).

---

### 5. Guided Practice (with hints)

4–6 items, e.g.:

- `4 + 3 = ?`  
  - Hint: start at 4, count 3 steps forward.  
- `9 − 4 = ?`  
  - Hint: imagine 9 dots; cross out 4.  
- `? + 2 = 7`  
  - Hint: think “7 − 2”.  
- `15 − ? = 9`  
  - Hint: “What number do I subtract from 15 to land on 9?”

---

### 6. Independent Practice

6–10 items mixing:

- Straight calculations (e.g., `6 + 5`, `10 − 3`, `14 − 6`).  
- Missing-number problems (e.g., `? + 4 = 11`).  
- 1–2 “explain your thinking” prompts:
  - “Why is subtraction called the inverse of addition?”

### 8. CONNECTION FORWARD / ENGINEERING GLIMPSE

**Mini Project: Balance Scale & Fair Trade**

1. Draw a simple balance scale with two pans.  
2. Under it, write an equation like `4 + 3 = 7`.  
3. On the left pan, sketch 4 small weights and 3 more.  
   On the right pan, sketch 7 weights.  
4. Then:
   - Add 2 weights to **only one** side. What happens?  
   - Add 2 weights to **both** sides. What happens now?  
5. Discuss or write:
   - “Why does doing the same thing to both sides keep the scale (and equation) balanced?”  
   - Connect to:
     - Weighing goods fairly in a market.  
     - Balancing forces on a seesaw or simple beam.

---

### 9. MINI QUIZ

**9.1 Quiz (3–5 questions)**  
_Examples (to be expanded by agent or teacher):_

1. True or False: Subtraction always makes a number smaller.  
2. Fill in the blank: `6 + 3 = 9`, so `9 − 3 = __`.  
3. Write a fact family using the numbers 2, 5, and 7.  
4. Explain in one sentence: What does it mean for two sides of an equation to be in balance?

**9.2 Answer Key (short form)**  

1. Sometimes false (if subtracting a negative number in later lessons; for Level 1, emphasize “usually smaller” for positive integers).  
2. `6`  
3. `2 + 5 = 7`, `5 + 2 = 7`, `7 − 2 = 5`, `7 − 5 = 2`  
4. Both sides represent the same quantity.
