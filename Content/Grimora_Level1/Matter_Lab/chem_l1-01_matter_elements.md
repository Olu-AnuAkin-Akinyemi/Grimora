## 1. Lesson Snapshot

**Title**  
What Is Matter? Stuff, Space, and States

**One-Sentence Summary**  
Matter is anything that has mass and takes up space, and recognizing what counts as matter is the first step toward understanding chemistry and engineering.

**Learning Objectives – Learners will be able to…**

- State a simple definition of matter.  
- Decide whether common examples (air, rock, light, sound, water) are matter or not, and explain why.  
- Describe the three main everyday states of matter (solid, liquid, gas) using simple properties.  
- Connect everyday transformations (melting, boiling, freezing) to the idea that matter changes state, not identity (for simple examples).  

**Key Terms**

- **Matter** – Anything that has mass and takes up space (volume).  
- **Mass** – How much “stuff” there is in an object.  
- **Volume** – How much space something occupies.  
- **Solid** – State of matter with a fixed shape and fixed volume.  
- **Liquid** – State of matter with fixed volume but changing shape (takes the shape of its container).  
- **Gas** – State of matter that changes shape and volume to fill the space available.

> [Deeper Study – Optional]  
> You can later add: **Plasma**, **particle**, or **atom** here for older learners, with a short drop-down explanation.

## 2. Narrative Hook

Imagine:

- A bowl of ice cubes on a warm table. Over time, the cubes melt into liquid water.  
- On the stove, that same water boils and turns into steam that fogs the window.  
- The steam disappears into the air, yet something is still there — you can feel warmth and pressure.

A Kimia mentor in the **Matter Lab** asks:

> “The shape changes, the feel changes, even the way you see it changes.  
> But what’s really there through all of this?  
> What do we mean by **matter**, the ‘stuff’ of the world?”

This lesson begins answering that question.

**Why This Matters (Now & Later)**

- **Daily life:**  
  - Helps you understand cooking, weather, breathing, and basic materials (metal, plastic, glass).  
- **Future chemistry & engineering:**  
  - Knowing what is and isn’t matter is the start of:
    - Balancing reactions,  
    - Designing materials and structures,  
    - Understanding engines, circuits, and even spacecraft.

4. “Can you feel air even if you can’t see it? How?”

## 4. Main Concept Explanation

### 4.1 Intuitive Explanation

Start from experience:

- A rock, a book, and a phone feel solid and heavy: they clearly “take up space.”  
- Water pours, fills a glass, and splashes: it’s not solid, but it still “takes up space” and has weight.  
- Air is harder: you don’t see it, but:
  - You feel wind push on your face.  
  - A balloon fills up and stretches.  
  - You feel pressure when you dive underwater (air and water pressing on you).

From these clues:

> Matter is the “stuff” that can push, be pushed, weighed, and measured — it has **mass** and takes up **space**.

### 4.2 Scientific Explanation (Level 1)

A simple working definition:

> **Matter** is anything that has **mass** and takes up **space**.

- **Mass** tells us how much “stuff” there is (we measure it with scales).  
- **Space (volume)** is how much room it occupies (we measure it with rulers, measuring cups, etc.).

From this view:

- Rock, water, and air are matter.  
- Light and sound are **not** matter: they are forms of energy that move **through** matter.

> [Deeper Study – Optional]  
> For older learners, you can add a note about:
> - Everything being built from **particles** called **atoms** and **molecules**,  
> - But emphasize we’ll explore that fully in later lessons.

### 4.3 Worked Examples (3–5)

**Example 1 – Is Air Matter?**

- Question: “Is air matter?”  
- Reasoning:
  - You can compress air in a syringe with your finger over the tip.  
  - You can feel wind push a kite.  
  - It can fill a balloon and stretch it.  
- Conclusion:
  - Air has mass and takes up space → **air is matter**.

---

**Example 2 – Is Light Matter?**

- Question: “Is light matter?”  
- Reasoning:
  - Light does not sit in a container; you cannot pour it into a box.  
  - You don’t weigh a “lump of light” on a scale.  
  - It can carry energy and cause effects (like warming your skin), but it does not have mass the way a rock does.  
- Conclusion:
  - Light is **not** matter; it is a form of **energy**.

---

**Example 3 – States of Matter (Simple)**

Give 3 items:

- Ice cube → **solid**  
- Water in a cup → **liquid**  
- Steam above boiling water → **gas**

Ask:

- “What changes?”  
  - Shape? Volume? How it flows?  
- Show that:
  - Solid: fixed shape and volume.  
  - Liquid: changes shape, keeps volume.  
  - Gas: changes shape and volume to fill space.

### 4.4 Simple Models & Diagrams (Optional)

Describe or sketch:

- **Solid:** particles tightly packed and vibrating in place.  
- **Liquid:** particles close but able to slide past each other.  
- **Gas:** particles far apart, moving freely.

These don’t need numbers yet; just arrows and dots.

## 5. Guided Practice (with hints)

### 5.1 Practice Set (4–8 items)

For each, ask: “Matter or not? Why?”

1. Sound  
   - Hint: “Can you put sound in a jar and weigh it?”  
2. Smoke  
   - Hint: “Is smoke just color, or tiny particles in air?”  
3. A metal spoon  
   - Hint: “Can you hold it? Does it have weight?”  
4. A smell (like perfume)  
   - Hint: “What is actually moving through the air to your nose?”

Then a couple of state questions:

5. “Identify the state for each: ice, milk, fog, oxygen in a tank.”  
6. “When water freezes into ice, does the amount of matter change? Why or why not?”

## 6. Independent Practice

### 6.1 Practice Set (6–10 items)

Mix of:

- **Short answer:**
  1. “In your own words, define matter.”  
  2. “Explain why air counts as matter even though you can’t see it.”

- **Sorting task:**
  - List: flashlight beam, sand, helium in a balloon, electricity, juice, a cloud.  
  - Task: Put each into “matter” or “not matter,” and explain one choice.

- **Explain your thinking:**
  - “Why do scientists and engineers need clear definitions like ‘matter’ when they design experiments or machines?”

## 8. Engineering Glimpse / Mini Project

### Mini Project: Matter Map of a Simple Scene

1. Choose a scene:
   - Your desk, your kitchen table, or a corner of your room.  
2. Draw the scene quickly.  
3. Label at least **10 things** and mark for each:
   - **What it’s made of** (wood, metal, plastic, glass, water, air, etc.).  
   - **State of matter** (solid, liquid, gas).  
4. Then circle:
   - One object that changes state in daily use (ice, water in kettle, hot pan cooling, etc.).  
5. Reflect in one or two sentences:
   - “How does this simple map help you see your space as a collection of materials engineers could design with?”

## 9. Mini Quiz

### 9.1 Quiz (3–5 questions)

1. Which of the following is **not** matter?  
   a) Air  
   b) Light  
   c) Water  
   d) Metal  

2. Which statement best describes matter?  
   a) Anything that shines  
   b) Anything that has mass and takes up space  
   c) Anything that moves  
   d) Anything that we can see  

3. In which state of matter does a substance keep its volume but change shape to fit the container?  
   a) Solid  
   b) Liquid  
   c) Gas  

4. Short answer: “Explain why air is considered matter.”

### 9.2 Answer Key

1. **b) Light** – Light is energy, not matter.  
2. **b)** – This is the standard working definition of matter.  
3. **b) Liquid** – Liquids change shape but keep volume.  
4. Air has mass (it can be weighed and compressed) and takes up space (fills a balloon, presses on surfaces), so it counts as matter.
